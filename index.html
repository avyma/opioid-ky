About the Project

Initially, the idea for the project was conceived from headline news about the extent of the drug crisis in the US that happened to coincide with the Lab 10 - final assignment for MAP671. Eventually, through personal interests and research, this has prompted initiatives to interview medical professionals, community leaders to understand the background and then how best to design and structure this project. Although Kentucky is regarded as the epicenter of the crisis, the availability of public data seemed lacking especially when compared to other states. Most unfortunate and astounding is the fact that data were suppressed for those counties with opioid related deaths of less than 5 per year.

Although generally known nationally as the "opioid crisis", deaths relating to other dangerous drugs are just as significant if not more impactful especially considering the characteristic of drug-used and choices of drugs seemed to correlate to geographical area in the country, income and age groups. Regrettably, because of the scope of the project, mortality relating to other deadly drugs that makes up the drug crisis were not analyzed and mapped. The difficulty behind recording and collecting data on drug-related deaths as the result of multiple drugs bring into question the reliability of the data to truly reflect the actual impact of the crisis. Other drugs outside of this project, as noted by the Kentucky Injury Prevention and Research Center are as follows: 1) Heroin; 2) Fentanyl; and 3) Psychostimulants (with abuse potential). Lastly, to tackle the drug epidemic, expert has noted the importance of working with community leaders and health professionals at the local level. Availability of data on the impact and resource availability is critical for mobilization of external resources. Within a few days of working on this project, it is realized that in order to understand the impact and the response efforts, data at the county level is not enough. Data needs to be at least at the census tract level. This could be useful in designing cross-analysis with demographic census data in order to garner a clearer view on the income, education, employment efforts of the locality. To be sure, availability of data at the census level is not readily available to the public especially in Kentucky.

In sum, the experience in doing this project is a true eye-opener to the importance of understanding the tragedies of the locality that one lives in. The analysis and mapping exercise presented in this Lab 10 in no way do justice in providing a clear, accurate and complete view of the opioid tragedy here in Kentucky. Much more needed to be done. Deeper stories needed to be told. Better maps need to be imagined, designed and constructed.
